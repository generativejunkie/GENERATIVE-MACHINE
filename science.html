The rigorous physics, chemistry, and mathematics underlying algorithmic creativity

01 / Physics of Sound

Fast Fourier Transform (FFT)

The Sound Machine employs real-time Fast Fourier Transform analysis to decompose complex audio waveforms into constituent frequency components. Based on the work of Cooley and Tukey (1965), FFT reduces computational complexity from O(n²) to O(n log n), enabling real-time audio analysis at 60fps.

X[k] = Σ(n=0 to N-1) x[n] · e^(-i2πkn/N)

Discrete Fourier Transform

Each frequency bin k represents a discrete frequency band, mapped to visual parameters:

  • Bass (20-250 Hz) → Capsule scale and position perturbation
  • Midrange (250-2000 Hz) → Rotation velocity
  • Treble (2000-20000 Hz) → Color modulation and emission intensity

Wave Interference and Superposition

Multiple audio sources create constructive and destructive interference patterns, analogous to Young's double-slit experiment. The resulting amplitude modulation drives the dynamic behavior of generative capsules through:

A_total = √(A₁² + A₂² + 2A₁A₂cos(Δφ))

02 / Chemistry of Visual Transformation

Molecular Diffusion and Perlin Noise

Image transitions employ Perlin noise algorithms (Ken Perlin, 1983) that mathematically mirror molecular diffusion processes observed in chemistry. The stochastic nature of particle movement in Brownian motion finds direct analogy in noise-based image distortion.

Perlin noise generates smooth, continuous gradients through interpolation of random vectors on a lattice grid—similar to how molecules drift along concentration gradients in Fick's laws of diffusion:

J = -D · ∇C

Fick's First Law of Diffusion

where J is diffusion flux, D is diffusion coefficient, and ∇C is concentration gradient.

Parametric Distortion as Reaction Pathways

The 20 generative effects can be conceptualized as chemical reaction pathways, where the initial image state transforms through energy barriers (transition frames) to reach a target state. Each effect has distinct "activation energy":

  • Low barrier (Fade, Wipe) → Direct transitions, minimal computation
  • Medium barrier (Pixelate, Mosaic) → Spatial quantization, moderate complexity
  • High barrier (Kaleidoscope, Chromatic Aberration) → Multi-dimensional transformations, high computational cost

Thermodynamic Creativity: Entropy and Noise

Noise injection increases system entropy (disorder), expanding the exploration space—analogous to raising temperature in chemical systems to overcome reaction barriers. This controlled randomness enables discovery of novel visual states.

S = k · ln(Ω)

Boltzmann Entropy Formula

where S is entropy, k is Boltzmann constant, and Ω represents the number of microstates (visual possibilities).

03 / Mathematical Foundations

Noise Functions and Gradient Vectors

Perlin noise is constructed through:

  1. Generating random gradient vectors at lattice points
  2. Computing dot products with distance vectors
  3. Interpolating using smoothstep function: 3t² - 2t³
noise(x, y) = lerp(lerp(g₀₀·d₀₀, g₁₀·d₁₀, sx), lerp(g₀₁·d₀₁, g₁₁·d₁₁, sx), sy)

2D Perlin Noise Interpolation

Parametric Equations for Effects

Effects like Spiral and Vortex use polar coordinate transformations:

r' = r · (1 + 0.5 · sin(θ + t))
θ' = θ + k · r

Frequency-to-Visual Mapping

Audio frequencies are logarithmically mapped to human perception (mel scale), then linearly mapped to visual parameters:

visual_param = lerp(min, max, normalized_amplitude)
